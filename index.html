01 · Problem Definition

Heat & Flooding Vulnerability
in Dudley Street, Roxbury

The Roxbury and Dudley Street area experiences recurring exposure to extreme heat and localized flooding — producing environmental and public health stresses that shape daily life. These challenges are not accidental. They reflect the interaction of climate change with long-standing features of the built environment shaped by historic disinvestment, land-use decisions, and uneven public investment.

"Heat waves and intense rainfall events are becoming more frequent, but their impacts are unevenly experienced. Some residents, households, and streets bear a disproportionate share of these burdens."

— Reidmiller et al., 2018; Anguelovski, 2014

🌡️

Extreme Heat

Roxbury sees surface temperatures up to 10°F higher than greener Boston neighborhoods — a disparity directly linked to redlining, tree canopy loss, and impervious surfaces.

🌊

Urban Flooding

Aging infrastructure and blocked drains combine with more intense storms, flooding streets, basements, and parks — repeatedly, not occasionally.

⚖️

Environmental Justice

Because some households have fewer resources to recover between events, climate impacts build over time rather than occurring as isolated crises.

🤝

Why Participation Matters

Data and maps alone cannot capture how heat and flooding are experienced, for whom impacts are most severe, or which interventions feel meaningful day to day.

Why Stakeholder Participation Is Essential

In the Dudley Street area, residents hold critical knowledge about which streets flood repeatedly, which parks provide real relief during heat events, and where past investments failed or were never maintained. Engaging stakeholders ensures that lived experience and technical expertise inform one another — leaving space for multiple adaptation pathways to emerge from within the community itself.

Stakeholder Mind Map

Following Bammer's (2021) fourth approach to stakeholder identification, we mapped all groups across six categories: People Most Affected, Community & Advocacy Organizations, Governmental Organizations, Housing & Economic Actors, Health & Social Services, and Research/Project Support. The Research group — representing Northeastern University and the PM class — is deliberately placed to one side, reflecting that facilitators are not directly affected and should not occupy a central position in the stakeholder landscape.

02 · Photovoice Workshop

Understanding Heat & Flooding
Through Student Eyes

Photovoice is a participatory action research method in which community members use photography, captions, and group dialogue to document their lived experiences and translate them into messages for change (Bell, 2015). Rather than experts deciding what matters — participants show what matters. On March 9, 2026, we facilitated a 45-minute Photovoice workshop with 27 Boston Latin Academy environmental science students, facilitated by the Northeastern University Participatory Modeling class under Professor Moira Zellner, with BLA teacher Amber Balthazard supervising throughout.

"Photovoice is a way to use pictures and stories to show what life is really like — especially for people whose voices are often left out."

— Facilitator Script, BLA Workshop, March 9, 2026

⚠ A Key Finding Before We Begin

Our original proposal assumed students were from the Roxbury and Dudley Street area. The March 9 workshop revealed that students come from across Greater Boston — Roxbury, East Boston, Hyde Park, and Dorchester. Their experiences of heat and flooding reflected this geographic diversity, and extended far beyond any single neighborhood. Future engagement must account for this.

Two Prompts, Two Directions

Students received two photo prompts from their teacher ahead of the workshop and brought printed images and written captions to share:

Prompt A — The Challenge

"How do you experience flooding and/or heat in your community?"

Prompt B — The Vision

"What is your vision for the future of climate and neighborhood resilience?"

What Students Shared — Lived Experiences

Students described environmental hazards not as abstract policy problems but as disruptions to ordinary life. Their stories revealed that the impact of heat and flooding is felt as discomfort and inconvenience rather than acute danger — a nuance technical data would miss entirely.

Negative Experience

Urban Heat During Everyday Activities

Students described heat making soccer games at the BU field unbearable, a golf outing with family turning "uncomfortable and bothersome," and BLA's own central heating system — which cannot be adjusted — making the school itself hotter than surrounding neighborhoods.

Negative Experience

Flooding Across the City

A student described flooding during summer camp in Newton. Another recalled heavy rain causing flooding in Downtown Boston two years prior. Multiple students noted that heavy rains bring more trash onto streets — a compounding hazard rarely captured in environmental datasets.

Negative Experience

The Sneaker Story — A Moment That Resonated

One student's photo of a flooded street sparked immediate recognition across groups. They had saved up for new sneakers, only to face a choice the next morning: walk through the floodwater and ruin them, or take the long route and arrive late to school. "This happens every time it rains hard. We just accept it. But why should we?" This story confirmed that local, relatable examples surface truths that surveys cannot.

Positive Experience

Green Spaces as Refuge

A student shared photos of the Emerald Necklace as a place of relief from heat — a park where the walking paths are wide, shade is real, and the design around the pond makes it "peaceful." Green spaces near Ashmont MBTA station were also mentioned as making the area feel safer and more comfortable on a daily basis.

What Students Envisioned — Collective Priorities

When asked about their vision for climate and neighborhood resilience, students consistently pointed outward — beyond their immediate block — to Greater Boston as a whole. Their priorities were clear and consistent across groups:

🌳 More green spaces in urban areas 🏟 More recreational areas in suburban Greater Boston ☀️ Solar panels and cleaner energy 🚶 Safer walking routes during heavy rain 💧 Stay hydrated — personal safety strategies

Students were not primarily concerned with personal safety from climate hazards — their experiences were framed as uncomfortable and bothersome rather than life-threatening. This is an important distinction for how interventions are framed and communicated to youth communities.

Workshop Timeline — March 9, 2026

  • 1
    Welcome & Framing — 0–5 minIntroductions, naming the problem, explaining Photovoice in one sentence, and setting group norms: respect, voluntary sharing, no right or wrong answers.
  • 2
    Facilitator Example — 5–10 minEach facilitator shared one of their own photos and the story behind it. This step worked well — students relaxed after seeing facilitators share personal stories first.
  • 3
    Personal Sharing & Caption Revision — 10–30 minStudents shared their photos and captions in small groups for both prompts, guided by facilitators who noted recurring themes. Students were invited to revise their captions after sharing.
  • 4
    Emerging Themes — 30–40 minGroups organized photos by prompt, identified common themes, wrote them on flip chart paper, and taped photos beneath each theme. Time pressure limited how fully vision themes were explored.
  • 5
    Whole-Class Synthesis & Closing — 40–47 minGroups shared top experience themes; facilitators linked student visions back to those experiences. Students were asked what action they felt inspired to take. Due to time constraints, student questions could not be taken at the end.

What We Learned — Lessons for Future Workshops

Test all equipment early

The whiteboard failed on the day. Facilitators adapted by using paper, but setup should always be tested at least 40 minutes before the session.

Confirm stakeholder attendance

DSNI representatives were unable to attend. Future workshops must confirm attendance in advance and plan how to share insights with absent partners afterward.

Build in time buffers

The 45-minute session felt rushed. Shortening the sharing portion by 5 minutes would create space for closing reflections and student questions — a critical part of the process.

All facilitators arrive 40 min early

Some facilitators arrived late, disrupting setup and group assignments. Early arrival must be a firm commitment, not a suggestion.

03 · Causal Loop Diagramming Workshop

Mapping the System Together

Causal Loop Diagramming (CLD) is a systems-thinking tool that helps communities visualize how different variables connect and influence one another over time — often creating feedback cycles that make problems better or worse. On March 16, 2026, we facilitated a 47-minute participatory CLD workshop with BLA students, building directly on the Photovoice insights from March 9. Students arrived already familiar with CLD basics from an online pre-session held the Friday before — a step that proved essential to the workshop's depth and pace.

"What are the causes of heat vulnerability in our community? What are the consequences of this vulnerability — and where could we intervene?"

— Guiding Question, CLD Workshop, March 16, 2026

💡 A Critical Design Lesson

The virtual pre-session held the Friday before March 16 was originally treated as optional. In practice, it was essential — students arrived already familiar with the seed diagram and CLD basics, which meant the in-person session could move faster and go deeper. This should be treated as a required preparatory step in all future iterations.

Four Workshop Goals

Goal 1 — Identify Factors

Name the main variables shaping heat vulnerability along Blue Hill Avenue, building a shared vocabulary grounded in student experience.

Goal 2 — Map Feedbacks

Identify how factors interconnect and reinforce one another — moving students beyond linear cause-and-effect toward systems thinking.

Goal 3 — Find Leverage Points

Identify where interventions are possible — at the individual, community, and policy level — and what trade-offs they involve.

Goal 4 — Connect to Action

Ensure the diagram belongs to students — a bridge to ongoing environmental justice organizing with DSNI and the broader community.

What Students Named — Key Variables

Students brought their own lived experience to the diagramming process. Beginning from a seed variable of Extreme Heat, they surfaced the following factors independently:

Carbon emissions Fossil fuel dependence Lack of trees & green spaces Urban development & buildings Transportation & traffic Air conditioning use Urban sprawl Animal population*

* "Animal population" was suggested by a student and placed in the parking lot — a facilitation strategy that kept the discussion moving without dismissing the idea. Revisiting parked variables at the end should be a standard step in future workshops.

The Reinforcing Loop Students Discovered

The most significant insight of the March 16 workshop was that students independently identified a self-reinforcing feedback cycle — without being prompted to find one. Tracing the arrows themselves, they recognized:

Seed Diagram

Starting Point — The Heat & Energy Trap

Students began with this seed diagram, introduced during the Friday online pre-session. It shows how extreme heat events drive up traffic and air conditioning use, both of which increase energy consumption and CO₂ emissions — which feed back to worsen heat. Blue arrows indicate direct relationships (variables move in the same direction).

Figure 4 · CLD Seed Diagram — introduced during the Friday pre-session to ground students before the in-person workshop

Student-Built CLD

What Students Built — The Wizard Wall

This is the actual causal loop diagram students constructed on March 16, using sticky notes and blue and red dry-erase arrows on the Wizard Wall. Variables include Extreme Heat, A/C Use, Fossil Fuel Use, Carbon Emissions, Carbon in Atmosphere, Amount of Plants, Likelihood of Heat Stroke, Fear of Going Outdoors, Less Physical Activity, Health, and More Infrastructure. Blue arrows show direct relationships; red arrows show inverse relationships. The circular symbols mark reinforcing feedback loops students identified independently.

Figure 5 · Student-Built Causal Loop Diagram — Wizard Wall, BLA March 16, 2026. Variables and connections contributed by students across five small groups.

Student-Identified Leverage Points & Solutions

During the reflection phase, students proposed a range of interventions spanning individual behavior, community infrastructure, and policy — demonstrating genuine systems thinking about scale and feasibility:

Green Infrastructure

Plant more trees for shade and carbon absorption. Create more green spaces and recreational areas in urban neighborhoods across Greater Boston.

Clean Energy Transition

Install solar panels. Increase renewable energy sources. Develop air conditioning systems that produce fewer carbon emissions. Reduce fossil fuel reliance.

Transportation & Urban Design

Improve public transportation to reduce traffic and emissions. Rethink urban sprawl and development patterns that displace green space.

Individual & Community Actions

Reduce unnecessary AC use. Spend more time outdoors. One student proposed this after a drive with their mom — reflecting on consumption patterns in daily life.

A Critical Trade-Off Students Identified

"You can't build more urban infrastructure and preserve green spaces at the same time — these goals are in tension with each other."

— Student insight, CLD Workshop, March 16, 2026

This trade-off between urban development and green space preservation was surfaced unprompted by a student — one of the most sophisticated systems insights of the workshop. It reveals that well-intentioned interventions can create new problems if the full system is not considered.

Workshop Timeline — March 16, 2026

  • 1
    Introductions & Framing — 0–5 minFacilitators re-introduced themselves, connected to the Photovoice session, and explained the day's purpose. Name tags helped create a peer-like atmosphere rather than a teacher–student dynamic.
  • 2
    Seed Diagram & Illustration — 5–10 minFacilitators pointed to the seed diagram projected on screen, explained blue (direct) and red (inverse) arrows, and invited students to build from it or start fresh. Starting variable: Extreme Heat.
  • 3
    Building the Diagram — 10–35 minStudents added variables via sticky notes on Wizard Wall boards, connected them with colored arrows, and traced loops. A "parking lot" held variables whose connections were unclear — to be revisited rather than dismissed.
  • 4
    Small Group Reflection — 35–40 minGroups summarized key insights on post-its: what reinforces heat vulnerability, what solutions might backfire, and what leverage points students wanted to prioritize.
  • 5
    Large Group Synthesis — 40–47 minGroups shared insights across the class. An important correction occurred here: when a facilitator began presenting their own idea, Prof. Zellner redirected — reminding facilitators that this phase must remain entirely student-led.

Lessons Learned — March 16

Make the pre-session required

The Friday online session was critical. Students who came in already knowing what a CLD was could engage immediately — without it, far more time would have been lost to orientation.

Facilitators are process guides only

During the large group discussion, a facilitator began sharing their own idea. Prof. Zellner intervened. Facilitators must resist the impulse to contribute content — their role is to draw out student voices, not add to them.

Celebrate closed loops explicitly

Students independently identified a reinforcing cycle but may not have realized the analytical significance of what they had done. Future workshops need a prompt to name and celebrate this moment.

Revisit the parking lot

The parking lot kept discussions moving without dismissing ideas. But facilitators must explicitly return to it at the end of the diagramming phase — parked variables should not simply disappear.

04 · Fora.ai / L-GrID Simulation Workshop

Designing Green Infrastructure
Solutions Together

On March 26, 2026, BLA students, DSNI representatives, Northeastern facilitators, and community members gathered for a 90-minute evening workshop using Fora.ai — an online participatory modeling platform hosting the L-GrID (Landscape Green Infrastructure Design) model. Working in three teams, participants placed green infrastructure on an interactive map of the Dudley Street neighborhood and ran simulations to see how those solutions affected flooding, cost, and community priorities. The workshop built directly on what students had documented in Photovoice and mapped in the CLD.

"There is no right or wrong answer. The point is to learn by playing. The nice thing is that we can try something on the map together, run the simulation, learn, and try again."

— Facilitator Script, Fora.ai Workshop, March 26, 2026

🌱 A Turning Point

When Vikiana from DSNI joined during the CLD recap and explained DSNI's work — inviting students to get involved — student interest and energy visibly increased. This kind of community partner intervention should be planned for and protected in all future sessions. It is not incidental; it is the bridge between classroom learning and real community action.

Five Workshop Goals

Goal 1 — Understand Flooding

Draw on lived experience of flooding in Roxbury to explore how flooding works at neighborhood scale through interactive simulation.

Goal 2 — Know the GI Types

Build working knowledge of green roofs, bioswales, and permeable pavers — how each functions and what it can and cannot do.

Goal 3 — Think Spatially

Apply neighborhood knowledge to make spatial decisions about where GI should be placed and why — connecting data layers to lived geography.

Goal 4 — Surface Trade-offs

Use concern profiles to surface and work through competing community priorities — and discuss who benefits and who does not from each solution.

Goal 5 — Build Concrete Strategies

Co-generate GI strategies grounded in both community knowledge and simulation evidence — for potential use by DSNI and community decision-makers.

How the Platform Works

Each team was assigned a green infrastructure type and given a $10,000,000 budget. They defined success using concern profiles — representing perspectives like a public official, a hydrologist, or a parent — then placed GI on the map, ran a simulation, and analyzed results across seven metrics: damage reduction, groundwater infiltration, maximum flooded area, downstream impact, investment, capacity used, and cost-efficiency.

🏠 Group 1 — Green Roofs 🌿 Group 2 — Bioswales 🧱 Group 3 — Permeable Pavers

Inside the Platform — What Students Worked With

Figure 6 · Fora.ai Baseline Map — BLA March 2026 project area. Red markers show flood-prone parcels with no intervention. Budget: $10,000,000. Boston Latin Academy is visible near the center on Deckard Street.

Figure 7 · Fora.ai Trials Results — BLA March 2026. All group simulation runs compared to baseline. Green badges show percentage improvement. Bioswales-2 achieved 88% damage reduction at $9.88/gal. Green Roofs showed 0% improvement across all metrics despite $9.7M spent.

Group 1 — Green Roofs

$9,744,000 Spent. Zero Flood Reduction.

The green roof group used 100% of available capacity and spent nearly the entire budget — and flooding did not change at all across any metric. Damage reduction: 0%. Flooded area: 0% improvement. Flooded streets: 0% improvement. Cost: $67.17/gallon — the least cost-effective GI type tested.

This became the workshop's most powerful teaching moment. Green roofs absorb small rainfall events and reduce urban heat — but they are not designed to handle large storm surges at neighborhood scale. They are a heat resilience tool, not a flood tool. Students began asking why it didn't work, which opened a deeper conversation about how different GI types function and where placement matters.

Group 2 — Bioswales (Best Performer)

88% Damage Reduction. Most Cost-Effective at $9.88/gallon.

Bioswales produced the strongest results of any GI type tested. Across two trials, the group achieved 50% reductions in maximum flooded area and flooded streets, and up to 88% reduction in damage cost — from $2,500,000 down to $300,000. The second trial (Bioswales-2) improved on the first by refining placement strategy, demonstrating that iteration and learning matter.

Bioswales were placed along Wyoming Street, Warren Street, Wabeno Street, Carlisle Street, Gaston Street, near Elm Hill Park, Intervale Street, Townsend Street, and Hazelwood Street — targeting impervious surfaces and flood-prone parcels on the baseline map.

The persistent gap: Despite best performance overall, Impact Downstream remained at 3% — unchanged across both bioswale trials. No GI type tested reduced downstream flooding at all. This is an equity finding: the interventions that most reduced local flooding provided no measurable benefit to residents further downstream.

Group 3 — Permeable Pavers

Improved Infiltration. Insufficient for Major Flood Events.

Permeable pavers improved groundwater infiltration but did not reduce flooded area or flooded streets in either trial. Cost: $23–$24/gallon — significantly more expensive than bioswales per unit of flood reduction. Useful at parcel scale for managing smaller rainfall events, but insufficient for the storm surges Roxbury experiences.

What Participants Told Us

  • 💡
    Iteration produces better solutionsBioswales-2 outperformed Bioswales-1 in every metric despite spending less — because the group refined their placement strategy after seeing the first results. Learning through the simulation cycle is itself a core outcome of the workshop.
  • 💡
    Unexpected results are the best teaching momentsWhen the green roof group saw zero flood improvement after spending $9.7 million, initial discouragement quickly turned into genuine curiosity. Students began asking why — which opened a richer systems conversation than any planned prompt had.
  • 💡
    Concern profiles made trade-offs visibleSparrow prioritized groundwater infiltration; Adrian prioritized capacity and efficiency. Having explicitly defined roles meant the group could name their disagreements and negotiate — rather than assuming consensus.
  • 💡
    Students wanted to keep goingSeveral students expressed interest in becoming co-facilitators for future sessions. Others asked to continue exploring in Fora.ai independently — including combining GI types and testing whether concentrated vs. distributed placement changes outcomes.

What the Simulation Cannot Capture

A central act of intellectual honesty in participatory modeling is naming the gap between simulation and reality. Facilitators raised three contextual limitations explicitly:

Land ownership

The simulation allows GI placement anywhere. In reality, the highest-impact sites are often privately or institutionally owned — requiring policy tools the model cannot capture.

Maintenance over time

The simulation treats GI as permanently effective. In reality, bioswales clog, pavers seal over, and green roofs need replanting — especially in communities with limited maintenance budgets.

Downstream equity

No GI type tested reduced downstream flooding. This is not a simulation limitation — it is a genuine equity finding about who benefits and who remains unprotected from the interventions modeled.

What comes next

The simulation shows what could work spatially. It does not show how to get it built — which requires organizing, advocacy, and policy change alongside technical planning.

Lessons Learned — March 26

Use Safari or Firefox

Chrome was unreliable throughout. Browser type significantly affected simulation performance. This must be tested and confirmed before any future session begins.

Book a computer lab

Reliance on student Chromebooks and personal laptops caused connectivity disruptions. A dedicated computer lab with stable internet is strongly recommended.

Show GI photos first

Students did not know what green roofs, bioswales, or permeable pavers look like in real life and were searching online during the activity. A 5-minute visual intro before opening the platform is essential.

Separate facilitator and tech roles

On March 26, facilitators performed both roles simultaneously — pulling attention away from participants at critical moments. Each group needs one dedicated facilitator and one dedicated tech support person.

Know students' day in advance

Students had taken their SAT exams that same morning — unknown to facilitators. This significantly affected energy levels. Check with the teacher about the students' schedule before any evening session.

Protect the break

The 5-minute transition break before Fora.ai was skipped due to time pressure. For future sessions this break must be protected — participants need a reset before the simulation begins, especially after a long day.
