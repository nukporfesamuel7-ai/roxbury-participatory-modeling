01 - Problem Definition

Heat & Flooding Vulnerability
in Dudley Street, Roxbury

The Roxbury and Dudley Street area experiences recurring exposure to extreme heat and localized flooding - producing environmental and public health stresses that shape daily life. These challenges are not accidental. They reflect the interaction of climate change with long-standing features of the built environment shaped by historic disinvestment, land-use decisions, and uneven public investment.

"Heat waves and intense rainfall events are becoming more frequent, but their impacts are unevenly experienced. Some residents, households, and streets bear a disproportionate share of these burdens."

- Reidmiller et al., 2018; Anguelovski, 2014
10°FHotter than wealthier neighborhoods during heat events
↑Redlining legacy drives tree canopy loss and surface temperature

DSNICentral community anchor and project partner

Extreme Heat

Roxbury sees surface temperatures up to 10°F higher than greener Boston neighborhoods - a disparity directly linked to redlining, tree canopy loss, and impervious surfaces.

Urban Flooding

Aging infrastructure and blocked drains combine with more intense storms, flooding streets, basements, and parks - repeatedly, not occasionally.

Environmental Justice

Because some households have fewer resources to recover between events, climate impacts build over time rather than occurring as isolated crises.

Why Participation Matters

Data and maps alone cannot capture how heat and flooding are experienced, for whom impacts are most severe, or which interventions feel meaningful day to day.

Why Stakeholder Participation Is Essential

In the Dudley Street area, residents hold critical knowledge about which streets flood repeatedly, which parks provide real relief during heat events, and where past investments failed or were never maintained. Engaging stakeholders ensures that lived experience and technical expertise inform one another - leaving space for multiple adaptation pathways to emerge from within the community itself.

Stakeholder Mind Map

Following Bammer's (2021) fourth approach to stakeholder identification, we mapped all groups across six categories: People Most Affected, Community & Advocacy Organizations, Governmental Organizations, Housing & Economic Actors, Health & Social Services, and Research/Project Support. The Research group is placed to one side, reflecting that facilitators are not directly affected and should not occupy a central position in the stakeholder landscape.

02 - Photovoice Workshop

Understanding Heat & Flooding
Through Student Eyes

Photovoice is a participatory action research method in which community members use photography, captions, and group dialogue to document their lived experiences and translate them into messages for change (Bell, 2015). On March 9, 2026, we facilitated a 45-minute workshop with 27 Boston Latin Academy environmental science students, facilitated by the Northeastern University Participatory Modeling class under Professor Moira Zellner, with BLA teacher Amber Balthazard supervising throughout.

"You students are the experts of your own community. You know many things that the City of Boston might have no idea about."

- Facilitator Script, BLA Workshop, March 9, 2026

A Key Finding Before We Begin

Our original proposal assumed students were from the Roxbury and Dudley Street area. The March 9 workshop revealed that students come from across Greater Boston - Roxbury, East Boston, Hyde Park, and Dorchester. Their experiences of heat and flooding reflected this geographic diversity. Future engagement must account for this.

Two Prompts, Two Directions

Students received two photo prompts ahead of the workshop. To ground the activity, facilitators shared their own photos and stories first — modeling the process before inviting students to share.

Prompt A - The Challenge

"How do you experience flooding and/or heat in your community?"

Prompt B - The Vision

"What is your vision for the future of climate and neighborhood resilience?"

Facilitator-Provided Example Photos

Before students shared their own photos, each facilitator modeled the process by sharing one of their own images and the story behind it. This step helped students relax and understand what was expected. The two images below are the facilitator examples used on March 9, 2026.

Facilitator Photo - Prompt A (The Challenge). Shared by facilitator to illustrate lived experience of extreme heat. This image was used to open the discussion about how heat shapes daily life and movement.

Facilitator Photo - Prompt B (The Vision). Shared by facilitator to illustrate a vision for green, resilient urban spaces. Students connected this image to their own aspirations for more green spaces across Greater Boston.

What Students Shared - Lived Experiences

Student Experience

Urban Heat During Everyday Activities

Students described heat making soccer games at the BU field unbearable, a golf outing with family turning "uncomfortable and bothersome," and BLA's own central heating system - which cannot be adjusted - making the school itself hotter than surrounding neighborhoods.

Student Experience

Flooding Across the City

A student described flooding during summer camp in Newton. Another recalled heavy rain causing flooding in Downtown Boston two years prior. Multiple students noted that heavy rains bring more trash onto streets - a compounding hazard rarely captured in environmental datasets.

Student Posters from the Workshop

Each small group produced two posters - one documenting their experiences of heat and flooding, one expressing their visions for resilience. These are the actual artifacts students made on March 9, 2026.

Figure 2 - Student Experience Poster - March 9, 2026. Themes: extreme heat during golf and soccer, flooding in Newton and Downtown Boston, BLA's overheating heating system, snow melt runoff.

Figure 3 - Student Vision Poster - March 9, 2026. Themes: green spaces, recreational areas across Greater Boston, innovation through solar panels.

What Students Envisioned

More green spaces in urban areas More recreational areas across Greater Boston Solar panels and cleaner energy Safer walking routes during heavy rain Personal safety strategies: stay hydrated, stay inside

Lessons Learned - March 9

Test all equipment early

The whiteboard failed on the day. Setup should always be tested at least 40 minutes before the session begins.

Confirm stakeholder attendance

DSNI representatives were unable to attend. Future workshops must confirm attendance in advance and plan how to share insights with absent partners.

Build in time buffers

The 45-minute session felt rushed. Shortening the sharing portion by 5 minutes would create space for closing reflections and student questions.

All facilitators arrive 40 min early

Some facilitators arrived late, disrupting setup and group assignments. Early arrival must be a firm commitment.

"Students from all over Greater Boston showed a good sense of belonging to the city as a whole. Their vision was not limited to BLA - they wanted a safe and healthy environment across all of Greater Boston."

- Facilitator Post-Workshop Reflection, Sajjad Sharifi
03 - Causal Loop Diagramming Workshop

Mapping the System Together

Causal Loop Diagramming (CLD) is a systems-thinking tool that helps communities visualize how different variables connect and influence one another over time - often creating feedback cycles that make problems better or worse. On March 16, 2026, we facilitated a 47-minute participatory CLD workshop with BLA students, building directly on the Photovoice insights from March 9. Students arrived already familiar with CLD basics from an online pre-session held the Friday before.

"What are the causes of heat vulnerability in our community? What are the consequences - and where could we intervene?"

- Guiding Question, CLD Workshop, March 16, 2026

A Critical Design Lesson

The virtual pre-session held the Friday before March 16 was originally treated as optional. In practice, it was essential - students arrived already familiar with the seed diagram and CLD basics, which meant the in-person session could move faster and go deeper. This should be treated as a required preparatory step in all future iterations.

Four Workshop Goals

Goal 1 - Identify Factors

Name the main variables shaping heat vulnerability along Blue Hill Avenue, building a shared vocabulary grounded in student experience.

Goal 2 - Map Feedbacks

Identify how factors interconnect and reinforce one another - moving students beyond linear cause-and-effect toward systems thinking.

Goal 3 - Find Leverage Points

Identify where interventions are possible at individual, community, and policy levels - and what trade-offs they involve.

Goal 4 - Connect to Action

Ensure the diagram belongs to students - a bridge to ongoing environmental justice organizing with DSNI and the broader community.

Seed Diagram

Starting Point - The Heat and Energy Trap

Students began with this seed diagram, introduced during the Friday online pre-session. It shows how extreme heat events drive up traffic and AC use, both increasing energy consumption and CO2 emissions - which feed back to worsen heat. Blue arrows indicate direct relationships.

Figure 4 - CLD Seed Diagram - introduced during the Friday pre-session to ground students before the in-person workshop

Student-Built CLD

What Students Built - The Wizard Wall

This is the actual causal loop diagram students constructed on March 16 using sticky notes and blue and red dry-erase arrows. Variables include Extreme Heat, A/C Use, Fossil Fuel Use, Carbon Emissions, Carbon in Atmosphere, Amount of Plants, Likelihood of Heat Stroke, Fear of Going Outdoors, Less Physical Activity, and Health. Blue arrows show direct relationships; red arrows show inverse relationships. The circular symbols mark reinforcing feedback loops students identified independently.

Figure 5 - Student-Built Causal Loop Diagram - Wizard Wall, BLA March 16, 2026. Variables and connections contributed by students across five small groups.

The Reinforcing Loop Students Discovered

The most significant insight of March 16 was that students independently identified a self-reinforcing feedback cycle without being prompted. Fossil fuel dependence reduces green space through urban development - less green space intensifies heat - more heat drives AC use - more AC increases emissions - which worsens climate change and produces more extreme heat. Future facilitation should include an explicit moment to name and celebrate when a loop is closed.

A Critical Trade-Off Students Identified

"You can't build more urban infrastructure and preserve green spaces at the same time - these goals are in tension with each other."

- Student insight, CLD Workshop, March 16, 2026

Student-Identified Solutions

Green Infrastructure

Plant more trees for shade and carbon absorption. Create more green spaces and recreational areas across Greater Boston.

Clean Energy Transition

Install solar panels. Increase renewable energy. Develop AC systems that produce fewer carbon emissions.

Transportation and Urban Design

Improve public transportation to reduce traffic and emissions. Rethink urban sprawl patterns that displace green space.

Individual Actions

Reduce unnecessary AC use. Spend more time outdoors. One student proposed this after a drive with their mom - reflecting on consumption in daily life.

Lessons Learned - March 16

Make the pre-session required

The Friday online session was critical. Students who came in already knowing what a CLD was could engage immediately.

Facilitators are process guides only

During the large group discussion, a facilitator began sharing their own idea. Prof. Zellner intervened. Facilitators must resist contributing content - their role is to draw out student voices.

Celebrate closed loops explicitly

Students independently identified a reinforcing cycle but may not have realized the analytical significance. Future workshops need a prompt to name and celebrate this moment.

Revisit the parking lot

The parking lot kept discussions moving without dismissing ideas. Facilitators must explicitly return to it at the end of the diagramming phase.

04 - Fora.ai / L-GrID Simulation Workshop

Designing Green Infrastructure
Solutions Together

On March 26, 2026, BLA students, DSNI representatives, Northeastern facilitators, and community members gathered for a 90-minute evening workshop using Fora.ai - an online participatory modeling platform hosting the L-GrID (Landscape Green Infrastructure Design) model. Working in three teams, participants placed green infrastructure on an interactive map of the Dudley Street neighborhood and ran simulations to see how those solutions affected flooding, cost, and community priorities.

"There is no right or wrong answer. The point is to learn by playing. We can try something on the map together, run the simulation, learn, and try again."

- Facilitator Script, Fora.ai Workshop, March 26, 2026

A Turning Point

When Vikiana from DSNI joined during the CLD recap and explained DSNI's work - inviting students to get involved - student interest and energy visibly increased. This kind of community partner intervention should be planned for and protected in all future sessions.

Three Teams, Three GI Types

Group 1 - Green Roofs Group 2 - Bioswales Group 3 - Permeable Pavers

Inside the Platform - What Students Worked With

Figure 6 - Fora.ai Baseline Map - BLA March 2026. Red markers show flood-prone parcels with no intervention. Budget: $10,000,000. Boston Latin Academy is visible near the center on Deckard Street.

Figure 7 - Fora.ai Trials Results - BLA March 2026. Bioswales-2 achieved 88% damage reduction at $9.88/gal. Green Roofs showed 0% improvement despite $9.7M spent.

What the Simulation Showed - Real Results

Group 1 - Green Roofs

$9,744,000 Spent. Zero Flood Reduction.

The green roof group used 100% of available capacity and spent nearly the entire budget - and flooding did not change at all. Damage reduction: 0%. Cost: $67.17/gallon. Green roofs absorb small rainfall but are not designed for large storm surges. They are a heat resilience tool, not a flood tool - a distinction students worked out themselves by asking why it did not work.

Group 2 - Bioswales (Best Performer)

88% Damage Reduction. Most Cost-Effective at $9.88/gallon.

Bioswales produced the strongest results. Across two trials, the group achieved 50% reductions in maximum flooded area and flooded streets, and 88% reduction in damage cost - from $2,500,000 down to $300,000. The second trial improved on the first by refining placement, demonstrating that iteration matters. The persistent gap: Impact Downstream remained at 3% - unchanged across every trial. No GI type tested reduced downstream flooding at all.

Group 3 - Permeable Pavers

Improved Infiltration. Insufficient for Major Flood Events.

Permeable pavers improved groundwater infiltration but did not reduce flooded area or flooded streets. Cost: $23-$24/gallon. Useful at parcel scale for smaller rainfall, but insufficient for the storm surges Roxbury experiences.

"Our best result reduced neighborhood damage by 88% - but look at the downstream column. It never moved. What does that tell us about who this solution helps, and who it does not?"

What the Simulation Cannot Capture

Land ownership

The simulation allows GI placement anywhere. In reality, the highest-impact sites are often privately owned - requiring policy tools the model cannot capture.

Maintenance over time

The simulation treats GI as permanently effective. In reality, bioswales clog and green roofs need replanting - especially in communities with limited maintenance budgets.

Downstream equity

No GI type tested reduced downstream flooding. This is a genuine equity finding about who benefits and who remains unprotected.

What comes next

The simulation shows what could work spatially. It does not show how to get it built - which requires organizing, advocacy, and policy change.

Lessons Learned - March 26

Use Safari or Firefox

Chrome was unreliable throughout. Browser type must be tested and confirmed before any future session begins.

Book a computer lab

Reliance on student Chromebooks and personal laptops caused connectivity disruptions. A dedicated computer lab is strongly recommended.

Show GI photos first

Students did not know what green roofs, bioswales, or permeable pavers look like in real life. A 5-minute visual intro before opening the platform is essential.

Separate facilitator and tech roles

Facilitators performed both roles simultaneously on March 26, pulling attention away from participants at critical moments.

Know students' day in advance

Students had taken their SAT exams that same morning - unknown to facilitators. This significantly affected energy levels throughout the session.

Protect the break

The 5-minute transition break was skipped due to time pressure. Participants need a reset before the simulation begins, especially after a long day.
