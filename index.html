Heat & Flooding Inequities in Roxbury's Dudley Street

A community-centered participatory modeling project exploring how extreme heat and localized flooding shape daily life in Roxbury — and how residents can drive change.

01 · Problem Definition

Why Roxbury? Why Now?

The Roxbury and Dudley Street area experiences recurring extreme heat and localized flooding, producing environmental and public health stresses that shape daily life. These challenges reflect long-standing patterns of disinvestment, land-use decisions, and uneven public investment that continue to structure environmental vulnerability.

"Heat waves and intense rainfall events are becoming more frequent, but their impacts are unevenly experienced. Some residents, households, and streets bear a disproportionate share of these burdens."

🌡️

Extreme Heat

Disproportionately affects Roxbury due to lack of tree canopy, impervious surfaces, and aging housing stock.

🌊

Urban Flooding

Old infrastructure and blocked drains combine with more intense storms, flooding streets, basements, and parks.

⚖️

Environmental Justice

Long histories of disinvestment mean these climate stressors land hardest on low-income residents and communities of color.

🤝

Community Knowledge

Lived experience holds answers that technical datasets miss — this project centers youth and community voice as essential evidence.

Stakeholder Prioritization: Power, Legitimacy & Urgency

Not all stakeholders can be engaged equally. Using the Power–Legitimacy–Urgency framework (Mitchell, Agle & Wood, 1997), we prioritized groups based on their lived experience, ability to influence outcomes, and immediacy of need. Those at the intersection of all three dimensions — Roxbury residents — are the highest priority.

02 · Photovoice Workshop

Understanding Heat & Flooding Through Student Eyes

Photovoice is a participatory method using photography and storytelling to help people share lived experiences and create change (Wang & Burris, 1997). Instead of experts deciding what matters — participants show what matters to them.

"In the Dudley Street neighborhood, students experience heat waves, flooding, and environmental challenges in everyday places — on their walk to school, in parks, on their streets, and around their homes."

This workshop brought together Boston Latin Academy students, DSNI community representatives (Vikiana Petit, Kalamu Kieta), and the Northeastern Participatory Modeling class. Teacher Amber Balthazard supervised and supported students throughout.

Student Photo Prompts

Workshop Goals

  • See their neighborhood as a source of knowledgeStudents are local experts whose lived experience provides insight missing from technical datasets.
  • Connect environmental issues to daily lifeBy photographing real places — streets, parks, schools, homes — students document how climate impacts show up in their world.
  • Practice listening, reflection & collaborationSmall group sharing and whole-group synthesis build skills in observation, storytelling, and collective problem-solving.
  • Contribute knowledge that shapes planningStudent insights directly feed into the CLD workshop and future participatory planning efforts with DSNI.

"You students are the experts of your own community. You know many things that the city of Boston might have no idea about." — Facilitator Script

03 · Causal Loop Diagramming Workshop

Mapping the System Together

Participatory CLD is a systems-thinking approach that helps community members understand how different factors connect within a complex problem. CLDs show how causes and effects influence one another over time — often creating cycles that make problems better or worse.

"In Roxbury, heat and flooding are closely connected to housing conditions, access to green space, aging infrastructure, and long histories of disinvestment — they form a dynamic system where one problem often intensifies another."

Guiding Question

"What makes heat and flooding better or worse in our neighborhood?"

Four Key Stories From Our CLD

Our preliminary CLD — developed through team brainstorming and refined with student input — surfaces four interconnected system narratives. View the diagrams below:

Figure 4 · The Urban Heat Island & Energy Trap — Reinforcing Loop
Figure 5 · Full Participatory CLD — Heat, Flooding & Community Resilience in Roxbury
REINFORCING LOOP

Story 1 · The Infrastructure & Flooding Chain

Old infrastructure leads to blocked drains, worsened by trash accumulation. Urban flooding causes housing damage and mold, draining household expenses and driving displacement.

Old Infrastructure → Blocked Drains → Urban Flooding → Housing Damage → Expenses → Displacement
VICIOUS CYCLE

Story 2 · The Urban Heat Island & Energy Trap

Rising urban heat increases AC use, driving up electricity demand and emissions — which feed back into more urban heat. The "solution" eventually worsens the problem.

Urban Heat → AC Use → Electricity Demand → Emissions → Urban Heat ↺
SOCIAL IMPACT

Story 3 · Economic & Social Erosion

Low disposable income leads to delayed maintenance, reducing thermal efficiency. Flooding and heat drive up medical expenses, ultimately leading to displacement and weakening community cohesion.

Low Income → Delayed Maintenance → ↓ Thermal Efficiency → Displacement → ↓ Community Cohesion
INTERVENTION PATH

Story 4 · The Green Intervention — A Path to Resilience

Increasing green spaces directly reduces urban heat, lowers AC demand, and breaks the energy trap — while directly improving quality of life for Roxbury residents.

Green Spaces → ↓ Urban Heat → ↓ AC Use → ↑ Quality of Life

Workshop Structure (47 minutes)

  • Step 1 · Welcome & Framing (3 min)Introductions, thank community partners, explain CLD purpose and group norms.
  • Step 2 · Variable Brainstorm (15 min)Students write variables on sticky notes: What makes heat/flooding worse? What reduces them?
  • Step 3 · Drawing Relationships (30 min)Three layers — zoomed-in causes, zoomed-out connector variables, and narrative sense-making to identify feedback loops and leverage points.
